A far-reaching concept does not fit a finite mind in its native form. Not yours, not mine, not the very smart reader you imagine. To get it from out there into in here, you have to break it into shapes the brain knows how to receive. That is not a writing problem. It is a neuroprogramming problem. And the discipline travels — scripture, trauma research, software architecture, sermon notes, slide decks. Different rooms, same ladder.

I build the ladder in code. The runtime is [`sveltekitbook`](https://www.npmjs.com/package/sveltekitbook) on npm. Two scaffolders ship with it — [`create-sveltekitbook`](https://www.npmjs.com/package/create-sveltekitbook) for long-form pages, [`create-sveltekitslides`](https://www.npmjs.com/package/create-sveltekitslides) for talks. None of them write prose. They render it inside a schema that pre-decides what each page has to answer, and in what order.

The schema is `src/lib/outline.js`. A flat array of plain JavaScript objects, one per page. Every object has the same fields in the same order. The earliest fields hold the technical claim — the kind of paragraph a domain reader nods through and a non-specialist closes the tab over. The later fields are the same content in plainer terms, then plainer still, then the version a child could repeat back. Each step down the ladder loses precision and gains reach. The reader who lands on page 14 with thirty seconds gets the punch. The reader with five minutes gets all four fields and walks away with the structure, not just the slogan.

The ladder is the neuroprogramming. The brain does not store an abstract claim by storing the claim. It stores the picture, the setting, the parallel from somewhere else, the consequence. Translate a sentence three times into smaller mental images and the third version is the one the reader will be able to repeat at dinner, and the one they can rebuild back up into the technical version when they need it. The schema makes that translation a requirement of writing the page rather than an afterthought.

I learned this writing about scripture and trauma research, separately, before I noticed it was one mechanism. The verse in isolation is vapor. Who wrote it, who first heard it, what was happening that month in that town — that is where the meaning locks in. A child reading "blessed are the poor" without the cultural setting hears charity language. With the setting, it is a sentence said to people who knew exactly which Roman tax collector lived around the corner and how much he took yesterday. The setting is the field the verse has to sit inside before the claim is allowed to land.

Trauma research is the same mechanism in a different lab coat. The finding is the smallest part of the study. The population is the bigger part — who they were, where they were, what was happening to them. A study on attachment styles in middle-class American children does not generalize to children whose neighborhood was shelled six months ago. If a book gives you the finding without the population, the book is selling you the finding. If it gives you the population first and the finding second, you can take or leave the finding.

So the field schema has a setting slot. The page renders it before the claim. The schema is the harness — not a prompt, not a system message, a cage in the rendered output that forces whoever is writing the page, human or model, to put the cultural and empirical anchor down before the punchline. If the field is empty, the page looks broken on screen. The discipline is built into the layout so the writer does not have to remember to. And here is where the LLM enters the picture cleanly, not as the protagonist. The schema does the same job for a model that it does for me. Ask the model for a chapter and you get a sermon. Ask it to fill the technical field, the lay translation, the setting, and the cost — in that order, with no other freedom — and you get something I can edit with a pencil in twenty minutes.

The slide ecosystem reuses the same ladder. `create-sveltekitslides` ships a deck where every slide is a long page. Above the fold is what the projector shows: the public claim, in technical voice for a roomful of engineers. Below the fold, scrolling down, is the rehearsal note: the same content again, in plainer voice, in fuller setting, with the things you would say only if asked. Same harness, different audience per rung. The presenter walks down the page during rehearsal and back up during the talk. The reader at home gets both halves at the same URL.

The reader benefit is bigger than the writer benefit. A book with consistent fields teaches the reader the geometry once and rewards them with it for the rest of the book. Page 4 has its claim where page 87 has its claim. The setting paragraph sits in the same place every time. By page three the eye knows where the punch will land. The reader can scan a chapter for one slot or read across the whole book through one slot. Index becomes navigation, and the spectrum or timeline across the top becomes an instrument because the shape underneath is uniform.

This is also why I keep the runtime small. `sveltekitbook` exposes four things — gestures, an inline-markdown helper, a palette, a comments component. Everything else — routes, layout, contents, glossary — gets scaffolded into the project as editable code. The ladder has to stay stable. The surface around it should be yours to bend. The runtime is small enough that I can answer for every line, and the book around it is yours to change.

The pattern travels. Documentation wants the same ladder — claim, example, gotcha, when-not-to-use — on every page. Research wants population, method, finding, limit. Sermon notes want text, setting, tension, application. The contents change. The rungs do not.

If you write with an LLM, design the rungs before you ask for content. The chapter the reader can hold is the chapter the schema let down.
